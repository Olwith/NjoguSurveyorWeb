What is the Process of Land Succession in Kenya?

April 25, 2026 9 min read Njogu Surveyor Team
Back to Blog

Land succession, commonly referred to as inheritance, is the legal process of transferring a deceased person's land to their rightful beneficiaries. In Kenya, this process is governed primarily by the Law of Succession Act (Cap 160) of the Laws of Kenya. While it may seem straightforward, succession can become complex, especially when there is no will, multiple beneficiaries, or disputes among family members.

This guide explains the step-by-step process of land succession in Kenya, the required documents, and common challenges to watch out for.

Types of Succession

There are two main types of succession recognized under Kenyan law:

Type Description Key Document
Testate Succession The deceased left a valid Will stating how their property should be distributed. Grant of Probate
Intestate Succession The deceased did not leave a Will. Distribution follows statutory rules. Letters of Administration
Did you know? In Kenya, the High Court handles succession matters for estates with a gross value exceeding KSh 15 million or those involving complex disputes, while Magistrates Courts handle smaller estates.

Step-by-Step Process

The succession process typically takes between 6 months to 1 year, depending on the complexity of the estate and court backlogs.

Step 1: Conduct a Search and Gather Documents

Start by conducting an official search at the Lands Registry to confirm the details of the property and verify that the deceased was indeed the registered owner. Gather the following documents:

  • Original Title Deed(s)
  • Death Certificate of the deceased
  • ID/Passport copies of the deceased and all beneficiaries
  • Marriage Certificate (if applicable)
  • Children's Birth Certificates
  • Will (if available)

Step 2: File a Petition in Court

A beneficiary or the executor named in the Will must file a petition for a Grant of Probate (if there is a Will) or Letters of Administration (if there is no Will). This is done through the court registry or the Ardhisasa portal. You will need to fill out a petition form, draft an inventory of assets, and pay the court filing fees.

Step 3: Court Hearing and Grant Issuance

The court will schedule a hearing. If there are no objections from other family members, the court issues a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration. This document gives the administrator(s) the legal authority to manage and distribute the deceased's assets.

Step 4: Confirmation of Grant

The initial grant is temporary. The administrator must apply for a Confirmation of Grant within one year. At this stage, the court ensures that all debts of the estate have been paid and that the distribution proposed aligns with the Will or the Law of Succession. Beneficiaries must sign a consent form agreeing to the distribution plan.

Step 5: Transfer of Title

Once the grant is confirmed, the administrator engages a registered surveyor (if subdivision is required) and a conveyancing lawyer to prepare the transfer documents. The signed transfers, along with the Confirmation of Grant, are lodged at the Lands Registry. The Registrar then cancels the old title and issues new title deeds in the names of the beneficiaries.

The Role of the Surveyor

If the land is to be divided among multiple beneficiaries, a registered surveyor must conduct a survey, place beacons, and prepare a subdivision plan. This plan must be approved by the Survey of Kenya before the new titles can be issued. Njogu Surveyor specializes in succession surveys to ensure fair and accurate division.

Common Challenges in Land Succession

  • Missing Beneficiaries: If a beneficiary cannot be found, the court may delay the process until they are located or notified.
  • Disputes among Family Members: Disagreements over who gets what share can lead to objections in court, prolonging the process.
  • Lost Title Deeds: If the original title is lost, you must apply for a certified copy, which adds time to the process.
  • Undisclosed Assets: If the deceased owned other properties that are not included in the petition, the grant may be challenged later.

How to Speed Up the Process

While court timelines are fixed, you can minimize delays by:

  1. Ensuring all documents are complete and accurate. Missing IDs or death certificates are common causes of adjournments.
  2. Obtaining consent from all beneficiaries early. This prevents objections during the Confirmation of Grant stage.
  3. Engaging professionals. A lawyer ensures the legal filings are correct, and a surveyor ensures the land division is accepted by the registry.

Conclusion
